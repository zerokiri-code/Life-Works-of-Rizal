Accusation of Anti Spanish Activities

When José Rizal was exiled to Dapitan in 1892, it was under suspicion of subversive activities against the Spanish colonial government. His association with La Liga Filipina, a progressive organization that sought peaceful reforms rather than violent revolution, became the basis for accusations that he was inciting rebellion. Although La Liga aimed for reforms such as equal treatment of Filipinos, fair representation, and justice under the Spanish law, the Spanish authorities viewed it as a threat to their control. While in Dapitan, Rizal maintained that he was no longer politically active. He focused on community improvement building a school, a water system, a hospital, and engaging in agriculture. However, the shadow of his past and his enduring influence among Filipinos in Manila and abroad kept suspicions alive. The Spanish government feared that he was secretly in contact with revolutionaries and might still be directing or inspiring insurgent movements, especially given his stature as a nationalist thinker. To this day, some historians question whether Rizal was truly apolitical during his exile, or if he quietly supported the cause of independence through indirect means. Regardless, his continued surveillance showed that colonial authorities never fully trusted his intentions.
