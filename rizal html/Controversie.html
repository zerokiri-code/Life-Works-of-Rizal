Controversie

Accusation of Anti-Spanish Activities

Rizal was exiled to Dapitan by the Spanish authorities after being accused of fomenting rebellion through the La Liga Filipina, a reformist group he helped form. While in Dapitan, Rizal claimed to be apolitical and focused on community improvement. However, some officials believed he was still secretly involved with revolutionary efforts.

Romantic Relationship

Josephine Bracken, an Irish woman, came to Dapitan with her blind stepfather to seek Rizal’s medical help. She and Rizal fell in love. The Catholic Church refused to marry them without Rizal retracting his criticisms of the church. He refused, and they lived together unmarried.

Accusations of Secret Communications

Despite being under surveillance, Rizal continued to correspond with people outside Dapitan. The Spanish authorities feared that he was still influencing the growing revolutionary movement. Letters and visitors were often intercepted or watched.

Conflict with the Catholic Church

Rizal's earlier writings, especially Noli Me Tangere and El Filibusterismo, heavily criticized the friars. Even in Dapitan, the Church remained hostile to him. The Jesuits invited him back to the faith, but he declined to retract his beliefs.

Medical Practice Without Formal Authorization

Rizal practiced medicine freely in Dapitan, especially ophthalmology.Although he was a qualified doctor, there were murmurs among the authorities and clergy about the legality and propriety of his medical activities. Locals appreciated his services, but Spanish officials used it to further question his activities.
